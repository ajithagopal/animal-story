KERALA

Kerala has been witnessing a sharp rise in human-animal conflicts of late. The rapid growth of human population and the resultant increase in anthropogenic pressures on forest and forest fringe areas have been attributed as the major reasons. Human intrusion into wildlife habitats and significant changes in land use and cropping patterns have also contributed to the evolving crisis.

The State Forest Department had identified 1004 conflict areas in a report published in 2023. A growing frequency of “incursions” by wild elephants, leopards and tigers in places including Wayanad, Kannur, Munnar in Idukki and Vazhachal in Thrissur have triggered massive unrest among the local communities.

Elephants have been involved in the highest number of such incidents caused by shrinking habitats due to various reasons including river valley projects, conversion of grasslands into plantations, forest encroachments and use of forest lands for non-forestry purposes like erecting electric lines and constructing railway lines and highways.

Other species including wild boars, tigers, leopards, bonnet macaques and snakes also find place in the list of animals that cause loss to life and property.

Major Conflict zones

The forest belts along Kannur-Aralam-Wayanad, Palakkad-Mannarkkad, Munnar and Erumeli-Ranni.

Nature of conflict: Moderate and Long term

Reasons for animal deaths

Electrocution, poaching, snaring, infighting, poisoning, and degradation of forest habitats due to the spread of invasive plants.

Major incidents

  • The capture and translocation of Arikomban, Palakkad Tusker-7 (PT-7) and Pandalur Makhna-2 (PM-2).

  • The ill-fated translocation of Thanneer Komban.

  • The loss of lives due to the wild elephants PT-7, Belur Makhna.

  • A tribal farmer Gopalan earning the sobriquet ‘Puli’ after killing a leopard in self-defence.

  • A tiger mauling dairy farmer Prajeesh to death near Sulthan Bathery in Wayanad.

Efforts taken to mitigate conflict

Physical barriers including solar fencing, hanging fencing and elephant proof trench; formation of rapid response teams (RRT); early warning systems; and replacement of invasive species with indigenous plants in forests.
