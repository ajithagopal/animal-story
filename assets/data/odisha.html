ODISHA

Odisha ranks among the top five states in India for its elephant population, yet faces severe human-elephant conflicts, particularly impacting rural life in mineral-rich districts such as Dhenkanal, Angul, Keonjhar, and Sundargarh.

Elephants, typically long-ranging creatures, are displaced from their habitats primarily due to mining and infrastructure projects such as roads, railways, and canals. Dhenkanal stands at the heart of this conflict, with a staggering toll of 130 elephant and 227 human deaths over the past decade.

Additionally, rural areas grapple with significant crop-raiding by wild boars, prompting farmers to resort to electric fencing around their fields, unintentionally endangering elephants. In the last decade, 125 elephants have perished due to contact with live wires, with 83 electrocutions deemed deliberate and 42 accidental.

Conflict zones

Hindol, Sadar, Mahabirod, Kapilash and Sadangi forest range under Dhenkanal district. Bantala and Angul ranges of Angul districts. Champua, Keonjhar, Barbil and Telkoi ranges of Keonjhar. Bargaon, Kuanramunda, Panposh, Biramitrapur and Barsuan ranges of Sundargarh.

Nature of conflict: High and Long term

Reasons for animal deaths

Poaching, poisoning, electrocution and accidents have claimed 333 elephant lives constituting close to 43% of total deaths. In past 10 years, Odisha lost 776 elephants on different accounts.

Major incidents

  • May 19 2023 Three elephants were killed by a train at Bansapani Behera Hatting, Joda Forest Section, Champua Forest Range, Keonjhar. The iron ore laden goods train travelling between Banspani and Jurudi hit a 22-member herd at about 7.25 PM.

  • March 13, 2023 A male elephant died while coming in contact with a live electric fence put up by vegetable farmers in Keonjhar district. The next day, a sub-adult makhna was found dead in a farmland.

  • February, 2021 Infection claimed lives of seven elephants in Karlapat Wildlife Sanctuary in Kalahandi district.

Efforts taken to mitigate conflict

Three Elephant Reserves - Mayurbhanj, Mahanadi, and Sambalpur - have been established for wild elephant protection. Since 2012-13, efforts have been made to conserve and develop fourteen traditional elephant corridors by demarcating, safeguarding against forest fires, planting food and fodder, fencing habitats, and offering alternative livelihoods.

Habitat enhancement projects, including fodder plantation, water body creation, solar wire fencing, open well fencing, solar street light, elephant proof trenches and stonewalling are being provided in the depredation prone localities. As a part of community participation in conflict management, village level volunteers called ‘Gaja Saathi’ have been engaged in elephant conflict prone areas.
